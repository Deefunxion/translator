# **The Builder of Modern Iran and Standard-Bearer of Palestine: "Rahbar" Khamanei**

Khalil Kawtharani

**Since surviving an assassination attempt in 1981, Ali Khamenei has been one of the foremost architects of modern Iran and its leader for more than three decades. His political legacy has combined the concept of the "revolutionary state" with raising the banner of Palestine in confrontation with the West and Israel.**

In 1981, the world was introduced to Sayyid Ali Khamenei: a man in his early forties who remained remarkably composed while delivering a Friday sermon at the Abu Dharr al-Ghifari Mosque in southern Tehran, even as an assassination attempt unfolded before the congregation through the explosion of a booby-trapped tape recorder. The attack left his right arm paralyzed for life, but his survival paved the way for him to assume the presidency until 1989, succeeding Mohammad Ali Rajai, who was himself assassinated.

Before becoming wounded, Khamenei had also experienced imprisonment—six times—under the Shah's rule. The victory of the Islamic Revolution later carried him into membership in the first Shura Council formed by Imam Khomeini. Alongside this role, his primary activity was co-founding the Islamic Republican Party-often described as "Khomeini's party"-together with Hashemi Rafsanjani and Mohammad Beheshti, who was also killed in a bombing of a party meeting in the same year as the assassination attempt (1981).

A native of Mashhad, the capital of Khorasan Province, he came from diverse backgrounds: an Azeri family tracing its lineage to Tabriz, with Arab Hashemite roots. His paternal grandfather had lived in Najaf, Irag. which reinforced his fluency in three languages: Turkish, Arabic, and Persian. His religious studies took him between the seminaries of Mashhad and Qom, with two years spent at the Najaf seminary in Iraq. He was known for his passion for reading and for Arabic literature and poetry. He was also influenced by pioneers of the Islamic movement, particularly Sayyid Qutb, translating several of his works into Persian. Yet, as he once recounted, this did not prevent him from weeping in prison when he heard of Gamal Abdel Nasser's death.

His presidency coincided with the management of the eight-year war with Saddam Hussein's Iraq. During that period, disagreements emerged with the leftist current within the Islamic Republican Party, led by then-Prime Minister Mir-Hossein Mousavi, culminating in Khomeini's dissolution of the party and the abolition of the post of prime minister.

Following the death of the founder of the Islamic Republic, Imam Khomeini, Khamenei was swiftly elected to the position of Supreme Leader (Vali-ye Faqih). He assumed the title "Rahbar-e Enghelab-e Eslami" ("Leader of the Islamic Revolution" in Persian)

after a constitutional amendment removed the requirement that he be a senior religious authority (marja).

Together with Hashemi Rafsanjani-who was elected president after Khamenei-the two formed a duo whose governance shaped Iran throughout the 1990s, before their paths later diverged. Under the Khamenei-Rafsanjani partnership, the early postwar years in Iran, emerging from a devastating conflict, were marked by the slogan of "reconstruction." Efforts focused on rebuilding infrastructure in cities and rural areas, supporting industrialization and domestic production, strengthening military capabilities, reducing diplomatic tensions, formulating a national security doctrine to replace a wartime posture, and achieving a relatively integrated-though tense—relationship with the global order. In other words, Khameneis leadership oversaw the transition from revolution to statehood.

What distinguished Khamenei's line among Iran's political currents, however, was his insistence that the state not shed its revolutionary character. He came to embody the leadership of the "revolutionary state" current within the Islamic Republic's diverse political structure, holding fast to the belief that revolution is an ongoing act.

This "revolutionary state," guided in foreign policy by Khamenei's slogan "Wisdom - Dignity - Expediency," sustained close support for resistance movements in Lebanon and Palestine-Hezbollah, Hamas, and Islamic Jihad. Under his leadership, relations with these

movements evolved in a way that aligned with Iran's national security doctrine rather than conflicting with it, in a reconciliatory approach. His ties with their leaders were notably warm, as reflected in recently released recordings of his meetings with the late Sheikh Ahmed Yassin and Hamas leaders, and others with the late Sayyid Hassan Nasrallah and Hezbollah's leadership.

There is no doubt that the cooling of relations between Khamenei and Rafsanjani, followed by the 2009 protests (the Green Movement), represented a fracture within the Islamic Republic's system. Yet Khamenei's steadfast adherence to the directives of his predecessor, Khomeini-backed by the Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps, the Basij, and the revolutionary current-preserved Iran's position as a stronghold confronting the West and Israel, shielding it from compromise deals. It was, however, this same stance for which he paid the price on the morning of the tenth of Ramadan.

In 1941, after Reza Shah Pahlavi aligned with Nazi Germany and refused to allow Allied forces to use Iran's railways, British and Soviet forces jointly invaded Iran and forced the Shah to abdicate in favor of his son, Mohammad Reza, exiling the former while the latter ruled under foreign tutelage. Iranian memory may recall that episode in light of what U.S. President Donald Trump has sought-namely, the removal of the head of Iran's political system, who led the country for 37 years. Whether such an effort succeeds will depend on who continues the path of that man from Khorasan, who helped move Persia from tutelage, weakness, and Westernization toward an exceptional leadership role in the East.

# **The Battle/Struggle of Choosing the Successor: This Is What Khamenei Recommended**

Hassan Haider

In political systems that historically emerged from revolutions, stability is measured not only by the strength of institutions, but also by their ability to protect the identity from which those revolutions arose. In the Islamic Republic of Iran, the position of the Supreme Leader constitutes the cornerstone of the delicate balance between the constitutional text and the ideological vision.

From this perspective, the importance of Article 111 of the Constitution emerges, alongside the criteria outlined by the late Ayatollah Ali Khamenei in his final speech before the Assembly of Experts in late 2024, as an integrated framework defining both the mechanism of transition and the determinants of selection at what is considered one of the most sensitive moments in the state's history.

Article 111 stipulates the procedures to be followed in the event that the position of the Leader becomes vacant due to death, resignation, dismissal, or incapacity. Its significance lies in preventing a temporal or institutional vacuum that could open the door to political or security instability.

According to the aforementioned article, in the event of a vacancy, a temporary body known as the "Provisional Leadership Council" is formed. It includes the President of the Republic, the Head of the Judiciary, and one of the jurists of the Guardian Council, selected by the Expediency Discernment Council.

This arrangement reflects the philosophy of balance of powers within the system, whereby executive, judicial, and supervisory institutions complement one another to ensure continuity in decision-making. The temporary council does not operate in isolation from the rest of the state's structures, particularly since the election of the new Leader remains the prerogative of the Assembly of Experts, thereby preserving constitutional legitimacy and ensuring that the transitional phase is governed by clear temporal and procedural controls.

Accordingly, Article 111 functions as a "safety valve" against scenarios of chaos, especially amid the current turbulent regional environment. It enables an orderly transition that combines legal decisiveness with

political prudence, sending a reassuring message domestically and internationally that the system possesses the tools to manage sovereign crises with stability and balance.

In addition to the constitutional mechanisms for filling the vacancy, the late Leader, in his final speeches, set forth criteria that were closer to "recommendations" for defining the identity of Iran's next Leader. These included four axes of both value-based and strategic substance. The first criterion is preserving the direction and objectives of the revolution. Khamenei's vision holds that leadership is not merely the daily administration of state affairs, but rather the safeguarding of the compass that defines the general trajectory of the Islamic Revolution, which was founded on a unifying project encompassing politics, economics, and culture. Any deviation from this framework, therefore, would amount to emptying the revolution of its substance. The second criterion is preventing deviation and regression.

Khamenei warned that revolutions throughout history may experience gradual setbacks if insight and vigilance are absent. Hence, the Leader's role emerges as a المرجعية ) authoritative reference) capable of distinguishing between tactical flexibility imposed by circumstances and the relinquishing of fundamental principles. The ability to detect deviation at an early stage is considered a guarantee of the continuity of the revolutionary line. As for the third criterion, it focuses on deep, heartfelt faith. Alongside jurisprudential competence and administrative capability,

Ayatollah Khamenei emphasized the emotional and spiritual dimension, considering responsibility in this position not a mere administrative matter but a "religious trust." This faith granted the martyred Leader steadfastness in the face of pressures, strengthened mutual trust between him and the people, and made their relationship one grounded in commitment to principles rather than transient interests.

As for the fourth criterion, it is dynamism and readiness for continuous movement. In a world witnessing rapid transformations in

technology, economics, and international politics, it becomes essential for the leadership to possess a constant ability to adapt-yet without compromising fundamental principles. In other words, the Leader must combine vitality in performance with the preservation of intellectual foundations, so that development remains a tool to strengthen the project, not to alter it.

Based on the foregoing, and in light of the aforementioned criteria, it becomes clear that the Iranian system seeks to achieve a dual equation: reliance on a clear constitutional text, and stability of identity grounded in strict value-based conditions, alongside a conception of leadership as a continuation of the Revolution's mission, not merely another link in a chain of political appointments.

Accordingly, the process of selecting a new Leader becomes a pivotal moment that brings together constitutional legitimacy and what might be termed intellectual legitimacy.

**Dahiyeh Relives the Day of the Martyrdom of Sayyed Nasrallah!**

Roya Qassem

For the second time in less than a year, a sense of loss hangs over the southern suburbs of Beirut. It is as though the area has grown accustomed to burying its loved ones—yet it certainly has not grown used to this magnitude of grief. Yesterday, after Iran officially announced the martyrdom of its Supreme Leader, Ali Khamenei, in an Israeli-American attack on his home in Tehran, residents of Dahiyeh recalled the date of September 27, 2024.

In the early hours of dawn, the area awoke to the tremendous news. Its residents spontaneously flooded the streets, chanting at the top of their voices: "At your service, O Khamenei." Some fired shots into the air in expressions of anger and condemnation, while cars draped in black drove through the streets announcing the passing of "the Master Leader," replaying speeches of the martyred Sayyed Hassan Nasrallah about patience in the face of hardship.

By daytime, Dahiyeh was not its usual self: light traffic on the roads, shops closed, balconies draped in black. Even Ramadan decorations were turned off, for the martyr is "our father," as one young man in the Jamous neighborhood put it, where a group stood waiting for a motorcycle convoy to set off in protest marches.

At four o'clock, the host of the mourning gathering, Hezbollah, called on people to assemble in Ashoura Square in the Jamous area "in loyalty to the great leader and in solidarity with the Islamic Republic." The call was enough for crowds to block all streets surrounding Jamous Street. The massive throng appeared like a single body, dominated by the

color black. With no space left in the streets, some climbed onto the roofs of low buildings to participate in the event.

On the ground stood mourners from all walks of life children, youth, and the elderly, including pager-wounded individuals and scouts-chanting in one voice elegies from the Ashoura tradition, renewing their allegiance to the "martyred leader" and vowing to the Israeli enemy that revenge for the blood of the two Sayyeds and all who preceded them would come, even if after some time.

After about an hour, the mourning ceremony concluded.

The mourners carried their grief and their promise of revenge and slowly left the square, while chants of anger and echoes of elegies lingered in the air—a scene where tears blended with a renewed pledge to continue the path forward.

# **Sayyed Ali Khamenei: The Man Who Refused to Bow to Global Arrogance**

Al Mayadeen Editorial Board

With profound grief and solemn reverence, Al Mayadeen presents this brief biography of the Leader of the Islamic Revolution and the Islamic Republic, Sayyed Ali Khamenei, in honor of his martyrdom on February 28, 2026.

In 1939, in a modest home in Mashhad, the sacred city of northeastern Iran built around the shrine of Imam Reda, a boy was born into a family of clergymen who lived, by any measure, close to the bone. His father, Javad Khamenei, was a respected but financially humble religious scholar, and the family's straitened circumstances left a mark on the young Ali that he would reference throughout his political life as proof of something essential: A life truly well lived is one anchored in conviction and the pursuit of what is just and righteous, not in the fleeting comforts or hollow pursuits of this world.

From childhood, Sayyed Ali displayed an intellectual appetite that extended well beyond the seminary walls. He was drawn to literature, poetry, and classical music, passions he would quietly maintain into old age, and his later writings reveal a man who understood culture as the very medium through which a civilization expresses its soul. His early education unfolded in Mashhad before he was sent, as promising clerical students were, to pursue advanced Islamic jurisprudence in the holy city of Najaf in Iraq, and later in Qom, the intellectual heartland of Shia scholarship in Iran.

It was in Qom that Sayyed Ali Khamenei came into the orbit of the man who would define not only his future but that of Iran itself, Sayyed Ruhollah Khomeini. Imam Khomeini was then teaching a one-of-its-kind, politically charged interpretation of Islamic governance, one that argued the clerical establishment had a duty not merely to guide but to govern the Islamic nation. For the young Sayyed, this was a revelation. He absorbed Khomeini's theory of Velayat-e Faqih, the guardianship of the Islamic jurist, and became one of its most committed disciples, a relationship of teacher and student that would reshape the map of West Asia.

His intellectual formation was not confined to classical theology. He encountered the work of the Egyptian Islamist Sayyid Qutb, whose writings argued for an activist, politically engaged Islam, and he was sufficiently moved to translate two of Qutb's works into Persian. He also engaged with the Pakistani-Indian philosopher Muhammad Iqbal, whose call for an Islamic renaissance resonated deeply. These influences produced a man whose faith was inseparable from his politics; for Khamenei, there was never a distinction between the two. Long before he held power, Sayyed Khamenei suffered for his convictions.

### **Imprisonment, revolution, and survival**

Through the 1960s and 1970s, as Iran's Shah Mohammad Reza Pahlavi tightened his grip with the help of SAVAK, a criminal secret police, Khamenei was repeatedly arrested and imprisoned for his political activities. He was detained six times in total, subjected to interrogation and torture, and sent into internal exile. These years of persecution were not incidental to his biography; they became its core.

He would later describe them as essential preparation, pointing to suffering that gives a man the moral authority to lead.

When the revolution finally came in 1979 and swept the Shah from power, Sayyed Khamenei was among the inner circle of clerics and activists who had paid the highest personal price for it. His organizational acumen in the years leading up to the uprising, networking between religious and revolutionary cells, disseminating Imam Khomeini's teachings in underground circles, earned him a position of real authority in the new Islamic Republic.

Subsequently, he was elected to the first parliament, served as Friday prayer leader of Tehran, and became one of the founding figures of the Islamic Republican Party, which sought to consolidate the revolution's gains against secular and leftist rivals.

Then, in June 1981, came the moment that nearly ended his life and permanently altered its course.

At a press conference in Tehran, a bomb concealed in a tape recorder detonated. Sayyed Khamenei survived, but the explosion destroyed the use of his right arm and damaged his vocal cords, leaving him with the soft, slightly labored speech that would become familiar to Iranians and his followers around the world over decades of televised addresses.

Those around him at the time recalled a man who accepted the injury with a composure that seemed less like stoicism than genuine belief. Here was a man who truly expected to be tested.

> **"One like me shall never pledge allegiance to one like Yazid," - Imam Hussein, quoted by Khamenei as the foundational principle of the Islamic Republic's defiance.**

His presidency, from 1981 to 1989, unfolded against the devastating backdrop of the [Iran-Iraq](https://english.almayadeen.net/news/politics/us-complicity-in-saddam-s-chemical-attack-ongoing-through-sa) War, eight years of grinding, brutal conflict that cost Iran an estimated half a million lives and became the defining trauma of the Islamic Republic's early existence. A war led by tyrannical Saddam Hussein and backed by the United States and the West to halt the expansion of the Islamic Revolution.

As president, Khamenei navigated the war's political demands, managed the tensions with Prime Minister Mir-Hossein Mousavi, a relationship of persistent friction that revealed much about Khamenei's governing instincts, and deepened his understanding of power as something that required not just ideological clarity but institutional control.

### **The Unexpected Succession**

When Imam Khomeini died in June 1989, the Islamic Republic faced the gravest test of its short life, transferring power from a charismatic founder to a successor without fracturing the system he had built.

The expected choice, Grand Ayatollah Montazeri, Khomeini's designated heir, had been dramatically set aside months earlier after a series of political disputes. Into this institutional crisis stepped Sayyed Ali Khamenei, whose elevation by the Assembly of Experts surprised many observers, including Sayyed Khamenei himself, who initially refused to assume the role.

Principally, he lacked the senior clerical rank that Islamic jurisprudence traditionally demands of an Islamic Leader. But Khamenei did not allow the circumstances of his appointment to define its character. Over the following years, he quietly and methodically built the institutional authority to match and then exceed the formal power his title conveyed.

### **A 36 Year Guardianship**

As Leader of the Revolution and Republic, Sayyed Khamenei held a quality of power that is genuinely difficult to convey through institutional description alone. He was simultaneously the commander-in-chief of Iran's armed forces, the appointing authority over the judiciary, the state broadcaster, the Guardian Council, which vetted all legislation and candidates, and the top commanders of the Islamic Revolution Guard Corps (IRGC).

Despite these powers, Sayyed Khamenei governed less as a micromanager than as a strategic compass. He set the ideological direction and the red lines; he resolved disputes between rival factions; he made the final call on questions of war, diplomacy, and the nuclear program. He never traveled abroad during his entire tenure as Leader, never gave an interview to a foreign journalist, a deliberate

isolation that projected an almost monastic removal from the daily compromises of statecraft, while through his teaching placing the Iranian state on a controlled trajectory toward development and independence.

His foreign policy was anchored in a principle he expressed as 'neither East nor West', a strategic non-alignment that in practice meant consistent defiance of American power, fierce opposition to "Israel", and the cultivation of an arc of allied movements and states across the region. Under his direction, Iran deepened its relationships with Syria, supported Hezbollah in Lebanon into one of the most capable non-state military forces in history, supported Hamas and [Palestinian](https://english.almayadeen.net/news/politics/-al-aqsa-flood-a-milestone---hamas-marks-38-years-of-resista) [Islamic](https://english.almayadeen.net/news/politics/-al-aqsa-flood-a-milestone---hamas-marks-38-years-of-resista) Jihad, and, in later years, extended its network to Yemen's Ansar Allah movement.

This constellation of alliances became known as the Axis of Resistance, and Sayyed Khamenei, with the support of his disciples, was its architect and ideological father.

### **Palestine: A Cause Elevated to Sacred Duty**

Of all the political causes Sayyed Khamenei championed across four decades, none was more central to his worldview, or more consequential in its material support, than Palestine. He saw the liberation of Palestinian land not as a regional conflict or a national grievance but as a religious obligation, the most important duty of the entire Islamic world.

In his 416-page book "Palestine" and the subsequent "Palestinian Referendum", he laid out his position with precision: all of historic Palestine, from the river to the sea, must be returned to its inhabitants through a national referendum that includes Muslims, Christians, and native Jews.

He was the leading voice in the Islamic world against normalization, calling such agreements vain betrayals of the Palestinian people. He hosted regular meetings in Tehran with Hamas leader Ismail Haniyeh

and Palestinian Islamic Jihad's Ziyad al-Nakhaleh, coordinating both strategy and material support. Under his authority, the IRGC Quds Force provided weapons, training, and tactical guidance to Palestinian and Resistance factions, enabling the development of their own rocket and drone capabilities.

The International Quds Day, an annual day of global demonstration for al-Quds liberation, established by Imam Khomeini, was maintained and expanded under Sayyed Khamenei as a visible symbol of Iran's commitment.

### **Ideology Committed to Paper**

Sayyed Khamenei was a more prolific intellectual figure than his Western profile suggested. His writings span Quranic commentary, political theory, cultural critique, and strategic vision. Taken together, his body of work amounts to a coherent ideological system rather than a collection of speeches or policies.

His earliest significant work, "An Outline of Islamic Thought in the Quran", establishes the foundational argument that runs through everything he later wrote. Islam is not a private spirituality but a complete social system, an active ideology with a prescription for governance, economics, and culture. "A 250-Year-Old Person" draws on the political lives of the twelve Shia Imams to argue that their seemingly disparate choices, war, compromise, martyrdom, silence, were all expressions of a single, continuous strategy to establish just Islamic governance across two and a half centuries.

"The Spirit of Monotheism" extends this argument into political theology, presenting the Islamic concept of tawhid, the oneness of God, as a political force that by definition demands resistance to any human authority that exceeds its proper bounds.

"A Discourse on Patience" reframes the Quranic virtue of sabr not as passive acceptance but as the active, willed endurance of difficulty in

pursuit of a just end, a reinterpretation that gave religious sanction to the lived reality of life under sanctions and siege of the West.

His 2019 manifesto, "The Second Step of the Islamic Revolution", addressed Iran's youth directly and laid out his vision for the next forty years of the Republic.

Self-sufficiency, ideological purity, resistance to cultural penetration, and the cultivation of a generation that would complete what his own had only begun were the main pillars of his vision.

His two open letters to Western youth in 2015 and 2016 went viral in some quarters, urging young Europeans and Americans to read the Quran for themselves rather than accepting a distorted version offered by their governments and media.

**"Islam is not a personal belief. It is an active ideology, a comprehensive prescription for how society must be organized.", Sayyed Khamenei, An Outline of Islamic Thought in the Quran**

Alongside these original works, his early translations reveal the intellectual influences he considered important enough to carry into Persian. He translated the Egyptian thinker Sayyid Qutb's "The Future in the Realm of Islam", a work that argued for the global ascendancy of Islamic civilization, and "The Peace Treaty" of Imam Hassan, a text he used to make the subtle argument that political flexibility and strategic compromise are not betrayals of principle but, when properly understood, expressions of it.

#### **The Theology of Resistance**

To understand Sayyed Khamenei's thought, one must understand the story of Karbala. In 680 CE, Imam Hussein, the grandson of the Prophet Muhammad and the third Shia Imam, refused to pledge allegiance to the Caliph Yazid, a tyrant who had corrupted Islam's

original message. Imam Hussein marched with a small band of followers into the desert of what is now Iraq, where he was surrounded and massacred. His refusal, and his martyrdom, became the central moral event of Shia consciousness, a template for the relationship between truth and power that Shia Muslims have interpreted and reinterpreted across fourteen centuries.

Khamenei presented Karbala not as history but as a living operating system for Iranian statecraft and resistance in West Asia. He quoted Imam Hussein's declaration, "One like me shall never pledge allegiance to one like Yazid", as the definitive expression of the Islamic Republic's posture toward the United States.

In this framework, America, under its current regime, is Yazid, a corrupt, tyrannical power demanding submission. The Resistance is Imam Hussein, outgunned, isolated, but morally undefeated.
