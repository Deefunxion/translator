## **State Development Through Self-Reliance**

The Iranian Revolution of 1979 and the Iran–Iraq War of 1980–1988 – a US- facilitated proxy war known in Iran as the Sacred Defense – were the historical foundations of the country's self-reliance doctrine. Endogenous military development coalesced with self-sufficiency in other sectors, such as industry and agriculture. The nascent Islamic Republic even enshrined economic self-sufficiency into its constitution (Abrahamian 2008). Although Iran lacked the capacity to instantaneously begin mass domestic production, the leadership's emphasis on independent development eventually bore fruit. Given its limited defense capabilities, the nascent Islamic Republic resorted to

importing weapons during the Sacred Defense. It procured arms mostly from Syria, Libya, and the Democratic People's Republic of Korea (DPRK). Iran received its first batch of ballistic missiles, Soviet-made Scud-Bs, in 1984 (Kesic 2025a). Missile import numbers were miniscule at the time, and Iran utilized covert smuggling networks to bypass a punitive embargo. Despite these restrictions, Iranian military experts routinely set aside one or two missiles for reverse engineering. Engineers dismantled the imported missiles, studied them, and began to build the core of a formidable missile arsenal (Al-Zein 2025).

General Amir Ali Hajizadeh (2021), head of the Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corp's (IRGC) Aerospace Forces before Israel assassinated him in June 2025, details the difficulties Iran faced at this early juncture. 'For instance, we obtained thirty missiles from Libya, two of which were set aside for production purposes. This occurred over two years, during which our stockpile never exceeded seven or eight missiles at any given time.' The demands of the ongoing war forced Iran to deploy most of the imported weapons shortly after receiving them. Iran also lacked the basic infrastructure to develop the missile sector and other areas. 'Back then, subsystems were simply not produced', Hajizadeh recalls. 'The technology for them didn't exist in the country. In order to achieve missile technology, we first had to develop several prerequisite technologies.' The costly immediate repercussions did not deter Iranian leaders from making decisions with a long-term outlook. When purchasing products, Hajizadeh (2021) explains, the seller 'might give you other things too in the subsequent stages, but after that, the entire approach becomes one of purchasing'. This would create a relationship of dependency. Under such a model, Iran would remain beholden to external forces and developments beyond its control. If an allied country fell, for instance, Iran would find itself even more isolated in the global economy.

In such a context, economic sanctions posed a major threat to Iran, both in its ability to import weapons and its domestic production capacity. Soon after the 1979 revolution and the ensuing hostage situation, US President Jimmy Carter levied sanctions against Iran under the International Emergency Economic Powers Act. Iran was the first ever target of this 1977 law, which granted presidential authority to impose sanctions. Washington subsequently froze \$12 billion in Iranian assets (Harris 2025). Iran's leadership nonetheless discovered opportunity amid tribulation. The sanctions, which were routinely augmented over the decades, compelled Iran to adjust its military priorities and to pursue alternative paths of development (Ahmed 2025; Mohammadi and Weaver 2025). There were difficulties to be sure. While Iraq attained weapons from its allies, both in the East and West, Iran was largely rebuffed during the Sacred Defense. By war's end, Hajizadeh explains, Iran lagged in military capacity. Yet the limitations imposed by sanctions spurred a national culture of resourcefulness and ingenuity. Led by Ayatollah Ali Khamenei, the political leadership recognized that self-sufficiency was a requisite for Iran's survival. Self-reliance thus became doctrine, and Iran maintained this posture even when opportunities arose to acquire weapons. Khamenei warned that overreliance on foreign arms would discourage and curb domestic innovation (Kesic 2025a). Only the latter could defeat the imperialist attempts to suffocate Iran in the long term. These early decisions proved crucial to Iran's historical development.

Political and military leaders also advised that Iran trailblaze an asymmetric strategic trajectory rather than mimicking the world's leading powers. They recognized the futility in pursuing fighter jets, tanks, and other conventional capabilities. Such a trajectory would keep Iran perpetually behind its most powerful adversaries and allies by generations (Hajizadeh 2021). Iran focused instead on ballistic

missile technology. The decision to exercise technological independence was not simply strategic, but indeed a political and even psychological imperative to combat imperialist pressure (Al-Zein 2025). Brigadier General Hassan Tehrani Moghaddam was the mastermind behind Iran's blossoming missile program. Ballistic missiles provided the only avenue to establish deterrence in a region increasingly dominated by Zionist-imperialist forces. But Moghaddam understood that deterrence was not simply about launching missiles. It was about 'mastering their lifecycle: production, concealment, deployment, and precision' (Al-Zein 2025). By the early 2000s, Iran began mass producing missiles at exceptionally cost-effective levels compared with their Western counterparts. Cost efficiency successfully offset the impact of sanctions (Ahmed 2025). At around this time, Iran also overcame its reliance on Soviet-developed Scud technology. With the innovation of the long-range Sejjil missile, Iran achieved technological self-sufficiency.

In the 2010s, Iran transitioned from mass producing long-range missiles to prioritizing precision and efficiency. Military officials also adapted Iran's storage and deployment capabilities. They established underground and mobile launch facilities to protect against the firepower of the US and Israel. By 2023, Iran possessed dozens of missile systems with strike ranges spanning from 200 to 2,500 kilometers. The latter can easily reach the Zionist colony, while the shorter-range missiles, more precise in nature, can target US bases in the region. Iran owes much of its regional power today to its ballistic missile arsenal, which is among the most robust in the world (Al-Zein 2025; Kesic 2025a). In recent years, Iran developed new military capabilities to complement the missile program. During the 2020s, it has rapidly advanced what has become a world-renowned unmanned aerial vehicle (UAV) program. US and European leaders even sanctioned Iran for allegedly supplying Russia with homemade UAVs

for its war against NATO in Ukraine (Council of the EU 2024). Iran also developed sophisticated electronic warfare systems, cyberwarfare capabilities, and importantly, a nuclear program (Ahmed 2025). Its nuclear and ballistic capabilities are mutually reinforcing, explains Abbas Al-Zein. While the nuclear program symbolizes Iran's sovereignty, the ballistic missile arsenal enforces it (Al-Zein 2025).

The demands against Iran indicate that even the Zionist- imperialist forces comprehend the mutually constitutive nature of Iran's nuclear and conventional capabilities, which together protect and advance national autonomy. Since at least the 1950s, US officials have underscored the importance of weapons standardization among Washington's clients, not to streamline efficiency but rather to monopolize weapons manufacturing and perpetuate dependency (see Foreign Relations of the US 1954a, 1954b). Whereas the US and Israel commodify everything from weapons to logistics, as Max Ajl (2024) explains, Iran distributes these capabilities with the understanding that strong allies are crucial for deterrence. For example, Yemen works to build a strong state under the slogan, 'A hand for protection and a hand for building'. Ansarullah's 2019 document, 'National Vision for a Modern Yemeni State', highlights its objective to serve the Yemeni people by establishing 'independence from any economic or political submissiveness'. The document stresses the importance of a 'modern, democratic, stable, and unified Yemen' that rests on strong institutions and a commitment to justice (Republic of Yemen 2019). Yemen's social programs correspond with defense capacity, or the 'hand for protection'. Ansarullah's coordination with Hizbullah and the

IRGC likewise facilitates military industrialization to strengthen Yemen's sovereign defenses amid a war of aggression by the US-backed Saudis and Emiratis (Ajl 2024; Sherif 2025).

The Axis of Resistance has collectively established a security doctrine based on self-reliance to combat US and Zionist regional hegemony (Ajl 2024). In this respect, Iran's national development, alongside that of its allies, translates to regional strength. This process in turn invigorates nation-building across states. Decades of sovereign capacity development enabled the post-Al Aqsa Flood regional war of resistance against the Zionist-imperialist forces. For Iran, the confluence of national and regional power was displayed during Operation True Promise III.

#### **Iran's 12-Day War of Resistance**

When the Zionist entity launched its unprovoked blitzkrieg on June 13, 2025, it banked on the swift collapse of the Iranian state. This calculation, assuredly shared by Washington given the latter's full support for the attack, was based on the perception of Iran's weakened status. Between sanctions, the notion of a strong internal opposition empowered by Israel's Mossad, and the view that Syria's fall curtailed Iran's regional influence, the Zionist-imperialist forces believed Iran was at its most vulnerable in decades (Lieber and Mulvaney 2025; Magid 2025). To be sure, the Zionist regime inflicted a heavy blow during its initial assault, including by assassinating multiple Iranian military officials and scientists (*Al Jazeera* 2025a). Yet Iran never came close to collapse, and the Zionist-imperialist projection proved a phantasm. The coercive mechanisms designed to gradually destabilize Iran, thus ripening it for military attacks, in fact created the conditions for state-building. Iran's national culture allowed the country and its government to endure 12 days of attacks by both Israel and the US. The state's indigenous capacity was tested during the Zionist attacks.

After the first night, however, Iran's air defenses repeatedly repelled Zionist incursions, both those launched from outside the country and those initiated by the Mossad's domestic assets (*Al Mayadeen* 2025a, 2025b).

In the early 2000s, Iran lacked a domestic air defense system and was susceptible to potential bombing raids. Russia offered Iran Buk surface-to-air missiles to mitigate threats at the time. Sanctions, meanwhile, limited Iran's purchasing power and its ability to weigh competitive offers. This juncture was a watershed moment, as the US had just initiated wars against Afghanistan and Iraq and intended to encircle Iran. But instead of continually purchasing from Russia or other allies, Iran's leadership insisted that the military research and develop a homegrown defense system (Kesic 2025a). Iranian experts spent two decades cultivating the country's air defenses, which dovetailed with its growing missile, UAV, electronic warfare, and cyberwarfare programs. They again transformed the threat of sanctions into opportunity. 'Sanctions have brought us many blessings', Hajizadeh explains. 'If we have our eyes on foreign equipment, we are always in need' (Kesic 2025a). The weakness that the Zionist- imperialist forces perceived in fact proved to nurture the very ethos which allowed Iran to endure the June 2025 attacks.

Conversely, Iran dealt a far more severe blow to the Zionist entity. Operation True Promise III inflicted the heaviest toll that Israel has ever suffered in its colonial history. If Al Aqsa Flood shattered the myth of Zionist invincibility, True Promise III exacerbated the entity's weaknesses, which were livestreamed to the world. The psychological trauma that the Zionist entity attempted to inflict on Iran, particularly through terror tactics targeting civilians, boomeranged and sent Israeli settlers scurrying to shelters. Iran relentlessly struck military targets, including those embedded in nonmilitary locations (*Al*

*Mayadeen* 2025c; Kesic 2025b). Decades of imperialist predations had the unintended effect of psychologically strengthening the Iranian population's resolve. This translated to innovation and ingenuity through scarcity. In short, Iranian resilience nurtured the capacity for development which in turn harvested technologies geared toward resistance. True Promise III distilled these historical processes into a brief 12-day window in June 2025, when Iran imposed a strategic defeat upon Israel. The 12-Day War highlighted Iran's emergence as a self- sufficient global power.

## **China, Russia, and the Contradictions of Dependency**

The historical demands placed on any national liberation struggle dictate that a country must be able to stand on its own. Iran, like any other country targeted by imperialism, must demonstrate national strength before seeking foreign assistance. No state will sacrifice its own resources and personnel to fight for another country that does not first defend itself. Iranian leaders have understood this basic premise of state- building since the 1979 revolution.

The 12-Day War simply confirmed Iran's calculus. General Hajizadeh compares Iran's situation with regional neighbors who have aligned with the US. While Iranian self-reliance and ingenuity fostered the development of a security apparatus that directly protects the population, other countries respond to regional security issues by soliciting Washington's assistance. 'Security can't be borrowed', Hajizadeh (2021) explains. 'Right now, when US troop numbers decrease in the region, [some] states express concern … Why? Because the power they hold isn't truly theirs – it is borrowed from the US.' Hajizadeh's point was validated when Israel attacked Qatar in September 2025. Qatar's US-supplied air defenses curiously did not activate during the attack (Ben Ari 2025). Iran's philosophy, on the

other hand, has propelled its regional and global status even amid far greater attacks meant to destroy the state apparatus.

Hajizadeh's words help explain why Iran did not immediately turn to world powers like China and Russia during the Zionist–US aggression. Iran's relationship with both states indeed mirrors its rapport with the AoR countries. During its proxy war against NATO, for instance, Russia has reportedly received shipments of drones from Iran, along with direct military assistance from the DPRK (BlackRussian TV 2025; Knights and Almeida 2022). In short, the countries that oppose US-led imperialism develop relations that are mutually beneficial rather than extractive.

Neither China nor Russia are imperial powers. They do not pursue national development by actively immiserating other nations (Patnaik and Patnaik 2017). Their relations with Global South countries are also non-hegemonic. Iran and other countries across Asia, Africa, South America, the Caribbean, and the Pacific develop ties with China and Russia while maintaining their independence with respect to domestic and foreign priorities. Whereas Washington sanctions or imposes tariffs on nations that transact with Iran, Russia and China pursue relations even with states inside of the US's imperial orbit. This calculation is indeed based on self-interest, but it also envisions mutual development.

The very structure of the Iranian state therefore precludes it from relying upon anyone else when threatened. In response to criticisms that Russia should have provided greater support during the 12-Day War, one Iranian official explained that Iran did not request assistance. 'Our relationship is a mutually beneficial one. Besides, the more you ask from someone, the more you have to give back in the future. We do not want to "owe" anyone any favors, as Iran determines its own policies.' The official added, 'Iran does not use its allies like a "milking cow" like some Arab monarchies do with the United States' (DD

Geopolitics 2025). Iranian officials, in other words, do not grovel to Russia or China and are not dictated to in the manner that President Donald Trump does with the US's European allies (Lory 2025).

While Iran coordinates with its regional and global allies, it ultimately possesses the means to protect itself. This sets Iran apart from its Zionist adversary. Within a week of suffering retaliatory strikes from Iran, Israel requested the US's assistance to attack Iranian nuclear facilities and even asked Washington to negotiate a cessation of hostilities. This reflects the reality of national liberation. As a sovereign nation, Iran retains the ability to determine its own reality, whereas the Zionist entity belongs to a corpus of imperialist countries that facilitate its occupation of Palestine and its ongoing genocide in Gaza.

**Iran: Reflections on War, Peoples, and Regimes**

Max Ajl

## **Taken from Middle East Critique. Published on November 4, 2025**

Since its establishment in 1979, the Islamic Republic of Iran has been at war: sometimes hot and kinetic, sometimes cold and economic, sometimes by dint of the radicalism of its own decisions, always due to the nature of the world-system and its US manager. In fact, the siege of Iran, its targeting for subjugation and destabilization, is inseparable from the wider US war against radical and independent nationalism. In the Arab–Iranian region, that war has recently taken a particularly vicious form. Iran, Yemen, Lebanon, and Palestine, and before their fall Syria, Iraq, and Libya, have been facing white-hot wars of US–Israeli colonialism, counterinsurgency, and de-development, as popular aspirations for dignity and development, often expressed through anti-Zionist commitments, face the heat of imperial violence.

The US encirclement and besiegement of Iran is best viewed through two perspectives: first, the overall US agenda with respect to any independent nationalist project, and second, the specific role of the Arab–Iranian region. Each perspective, in turn, requires a wider and more encompassing framework, namely an account of US unipolar imperialism – synonymous with the current stage of (late) monopoly capitalism. This relationship of worldwide domination proceeded in the Cold War period through trying to contain 'Communism', or independent nationalism, while the US began to develop specific comparative advantages: oil, weapons, and later finance, many routed through the Arab–Iranian region

## **Accumulate, Accumulate: That Is Moses and the Prophets**

Within the broader world system, different regions played distinct roles in unipolar accumulation during the early Cold War. Rebuilt Western Europe and East Asia became centers for integrated industrialization, the path Barrington Moore Jr. identified as

'modernization from above' (Moore 1966). This strategy in East Asia was coupled with agrarian reforms, and emerged more broadly in the shadow of China (Kadri 2021). Western Europe, likewise, developed in the face of indigenous, but often imperialist, labor unions, the mass legitimacy of Communism, and the consequent need to put in place a welfare state. Later, finance would be the mechanism to contain European independence, while military dependency would feature more prominently in East Asia, which nevertheless became central as a cluster of 'newly industrializing countries' (Gowan 1999).

During this early period, the US slowly took over the mantle of imperialism in the Arab–Iranian region from the British and French empires in their local twilight (Pigeaud and Sylla 2024). In the 'early' Cold War, the US engaged in state-building where necessary and sought the 'containment' of Arab nationalism where possible. Where nationalism became uncontainable through the early bid for natural resource sovereignty, as in Iran under Mossadegh, coup became the preferred option, followed by top-down developmental containment. Where this occurred under 'friendly' governments – Tunisia, Iran – the US used the carrot of aid, outsourcing the stick of repression to domestic neocolonial forces (Ajl 2019; Ayeb, 2013; Toubal, 1979). Where states seemed in the middle – Egypt – the US used 'short leash' developmental aid, lapsing into outright warfare and proxy hemorrhage of resources, through Saudi support for reaction in republican Yemen. And where states seemed out of control – Iraq post-1958, Syria post-1963 – the US chose coup d'etat and war. These were articulations of the Arab Cold War, where the US sought to keep the overall neocolonial structure under its grip, in particular for fear of a pan-Arab alternative architecture for sovereign control over oil resources.

Multi-vector developmental protections increasingly encountered political and social dissent resistant to the forms of immunity they had

created. At the same time, the US Pentagon complex, which fed off and fed warfare but had faced defeat in Southeast Asia, turned to the region as a major market for arms sales (Higgins 2023). Securing the political conditions for arms sales through reliable states without traces of regional republican tendencies, while ensuring the friction and violence needed to make arms sales reasonable, rational, and necessary, dovetailed smoothly with the Israeli hammer-blow to the Arab nationalist attempts to fuse distribution and defense in Syria and Egypt. More broadly, Israel, theretofore armed primarily by France and Germany, proved an asset to the profit-by-war agenda within US imperialism, which had seen its attempt to lull Arab nationalism into quietism in Egypt fail. War, then, became a prime vector of accumulation, justifying the diversion of surplus into defense and sowing defeatism (Ajl 2024).

Because capitalism, imperialism, and Zionism are expansive and opportunistic, unceasingly looking to expand their relative share of worldwide wealth, partial capitulation is never sufficient: 'Accumulate, accumulate: that is Moses and the prophets.' Privatize more, dislocate labor, swell labor reservoirs, burn them up as fuel in the

machinery of defeat, dislocate claims to common resources, these mechanisms all lead to ever-greater accumulation. Indeed, as Lyn Ossome writes, amidst varied forms of primitive accumulation, gendered labor in particular is 'engaged in a constant struggle against its own annihilation' (2024).

Accumulation in the Arab–Iranian states proceeds, now, increasingly, through state collapse in some states, and occasional postwar reconstruction – post-2003 in Iraq, post-1990 in Lebanon –whilst institutionalizing sectarianism or replacing state functions through expansive foreign-funded civil society and extensive 'security assistance', seeking to shatter then re-form states as gears nestled neatly into the US regional project (Mullin 2023).

In this sense, creeping neoliberalism under a system of overall US financial dominance, as in Syria pre-2011 (Matar 2016), was never sufficient from the US perspective, which seeks regional state collapse and privatization of remaining public commons. Nor is partial accommodation or obeisance to US security dictates – Muammar Qadaffi's partial disarmament in 2003–2004 under the threat of war and sanctions, or Syria's hesitance to open a front against Israel post-October 7 – sufficient from the perspective of US–Israeli power, which seeks total defeat. Indeed, Syria's partial accommodation, its overtures to secure financial help from the United Arab Emirates (UAE) amidst US economic siege warfare *via* sanctions, were coupled with the missile manufacturing facilities and supply lines to Hezbollah in Syria.

From this perspective, Iran has been a severe obstacle since its 1979 revolution. On the one hand, modern Iran is the child of a populist and popular revolt with a commitment to anti-Zionism, anti-imperialism, and delinking. This commitment is partly ideological and legal, as Iran has a constitutional clause committing the country to support Palestine, and partly pragmatic, the child of the 1980–1988 war with Iraq, and Iranian understanding of the need for technological self-reliance, as well as the notion of 'forward deterrence': forces fighting close to the Israeli armistice lines meant the fight was not meant to come to Tehran.

Furthermore, amidst a melange of ideological commitment and external coercion, Iran has moved to a pattern of defensive industrialization and partial import- substitution, with a particular focus on endogenizing arms production. Complementary to that policy has been greater capacity to circumvent sanctions by linking with the industrial powerhouses of China and Russia through selective technology import and, increasingly, alternative channels for smuggling, re-shipping, or re-flagging oil. As a result, Iran is able to

produce a substantial indigenous weaponry, particularly drones and ballistic missiles, paired with tunneling and other hardening or asymmetric expertise, and has extended that technology to other regional asymmetric and symmetric forces. This includes sharing blueprints with Palestinian armed factions, technical advisors with Ansar Allah, and missile technology with Hezbollah. In essence, these policies are 'economics neutral' – they do not rest on the redistributive policies of those forces. But this does not mean they are class-blind, because they defend not merely the Iranian state, but also the possibility of planning in Hezbollah, the nationalist and sometimes populist planning policies of Ansar Allah, and the possible creation of a state or popular reconstruction by Al-Qassam or Palestinians in general – depending on the outcome of a battle whose future is open (Mari 2025).

## **The Two Oppositions to War**

As the US–Israeli attack against the Islamic Rrepublic of Iran proceeded with the help of direct US intervention, those who championed opposition to the aggression increasingly separated into two distinct camps. One camp argued, at a minimum, for the defense of Iran, its right to defend itself against the imperial and colonial marauders, the right of Iranian self-determination, the sacrosanct capacity of the Iranian people to choose their government as they see fit, the defense of the Iranian state against predatory aggression, and for the US and Israel to immediately cease all military operations. Within that camp others indeed went further, pointing out that Iranian technological, logistical, and military support for the regional asymmetric militia, sovereign states, and popular movements is liberatory and just.

Meanwhile, though, another camp, for which the military terminology is the correct one, argued for solidarity with an abstract,

seldom-defined people, but never with the regime, never with the state, never with the Islamic Republic, and distanced itself from the IRI military, the apparatus of governance, and any other residue or container for Iranian-style practices: repression, authoritarianism, Islamic governance, sub-imperialism (Farnia 2023). This stance is misleading.

On the one hand, history has cursed us with social scientific–historical experiments showing us the use and misuse of selective solidarity with a beatific 'people' who allegedly are poorly represented, or punishingly oppressed, by one or another regime: Libya, Syria, Hamas in Gaza, and need 'saving' by Apaches, B-52s, and death squads under the moniker of 'revolutionaries' (Alqaisiya 2024). Furthermore, the US–Israeli media apparatus has long claimed to be targeting 'the regime' and not 'the people', the latter of whose casualties are collateral and regrettable. In the case of Iran, many are willing to condemn Israeli violence against civilian institutions, but not against the Iranian military. There is also a considerable reticence to defend the Iranian right to self-defense, inseparable from the military.

This brings us to a second point: what is a military, and what is a state? The fantasy that the state is pure repression, a kind of carnival of hopeless evil, drawing on US-based theories of abolitionism, is not coincident with reality. The state, as Philip Abrams made clear, is an idea and a set of institutions. The two are interlocked: the idea stands for the set of institutions in the minds of many, and the day-to-day practice of those institutions incants the idea of the state in the minds of its people.

In fact, the state is central to the reproduction of society. The state moves people around through publicly owned transportation systems, treats people in public hospitals, organizes national research projects, and decides which economic sectors will grow faster than others. Successful modern economic planning is inseparable from a state

apparatus, and state management and intervention in the economy is central to accelerated growth. Furthermore, people in Iran who are urging its defense, who rally behind the state and its actions, perceive the idea of the state as a banner, a means to defend all those things the state provides. In this sense Southern patriotism can be, and in the case of contemporary Iran certainly is, a working-class ideology.

A somewhat more charitable narrative allows for the civilian functions of the state in the South and certainly Iran. Attacks on hospitals, schools, power plants, and above all 'women and children' are rued – as though women do not, often, have husbands or brothers, and children do not have fathers. But the active mechanisms to defend hospitals, schools, power plants, cannot be mentioned. Attacks are attacked, but self-defense is not defended. That is, for many, the state – social security, the university, public hospitals – is allowed in Northern discourse and for Northern peoples but is disallowed for the South. Meanwhile, shrugging at the state also segues into shrugging at a ballooning Northern state–private-funded Southern civil society which assumes state functions of research and small-scale decentralized development, but in an ad hoc and decentralized way and without amassing planning capacity (Moyo, Jha, and Yeros 2013).

#### **Conclusion**

Such gestures are innocent of the actual history of planning and development. The history of catch-up industrialization in the Communist giants of Eurasia, the USSR, and the People's Republic of China is the history of seeking a balance between civilian-use industrial output and the need to direct surplus to self-defense. It was understood that to build an economy suitable for its people at a time of peace, one needed weaponry to protect the people from an enemy that would quickly and easily resort to arms. Self-defense meant, furthermore, an army.

In the specific context of the IRI's militarization, it is clear that build-up of a sovereign industrial capacity, often interlinked technologically with China and Russia, and producing more and more of its needs domestically, is the central guarantor of even the aspiration for a region-wide political sovereignty regime outside the US security umbrella (Ajl, Forthcoming). For Iranian self-defense is not merely local but regional. The IRI provides the possibility of weapons for the poor, which shares blueprints and gifts arms to the regional asymmetric militias or state forces like Ansar Allah, which are the only shield against US–Israeli genocide. In this context, the role of the state is inescapable, and the state idea is what connects peoples to that role. Indeed, it is precisely in the attack of the role of the state – christened 'the regime' as though there are no people who support the 'regime', and as though one can delineate where 'the regime' ends and self-defense or universities or hospitals begin – that US–Israeli propaganda operates to justify state collapse in the name of the people. To adopt anti-statist propaganda while disavowing is, therefore, normatively questionable and theoretically unsound.

# **US Imperialism is an Indian War**

Nick Estes

## **Taken from Middle East Critique. Published on October 31, 2025.**

The June 22, 2025 bombing of Iranian nuclear sites in Operation Midnight Hammer was not the United States' first foray into direct military action against the Islamic Republic or other regional adversaries. Despite no formal declarations of war for its many interventions in West Asia, the US has effectively been at war in the region for decades. Its vast military footprint – spanning more than a dozen countries, with ships patrolling surrounding waters and at least 19 military installations, 8 of them permanent bases – testifies to the depth of that commitment. Added to this are massive financial, political, economic, and military investments in Israel, all aimed at projecting US power and undermining regional sovereignty and autonomy. Yet a military presence alone neither explains nor justifies the era of so-called 'forever wars'. Indian Wars were the original 'forever wars'. They set the precedent for preemptive, unilateral, and so-called 'defensive' wars against peoples and nations the US deemed outside the bounds of 'civilization'. Long critical of this history, an anti-imperialist current within American Indian studies (Estes 2025) provides a useful framework for understanding US–Zionist imperial strategies in today's genocidal and expansionist wars against Palestine, Lebanon, Yemen, Syria, Iraq, and Iran.

Today's undeclared wars, whether fought under the banner of the Global War on Terror or more recently the so-called 12-Day War on Iran, do not merely resemble the Indian Wars; they inherit them. The continuity is linguistic, cultural, and legal. Military designations of enemy territory as 'Indian Country', the naming of weapons and equipment after Native American peoples and motifs – Tomahawk missiles, Apache and Blackhawk helicopters, or giving Osama bin Laden the codename 'Geronimo' – are obvious traces of this legacy. But more than symbolism is at stake. The supposed legality of the

Indian Wars has become a standing precedent for US interventions in West Asia, invoked to justify endless wars against any nation or group branded a 'threat' to US interests. This is the logic of the 'savage exception': whoever can be *made Indian* (or savage) is cast outside legal, political, and cultural norms reserved for 'civilized' nations (Aune 2023). What emerges is a permanent state of quasi-war, where the line between *war* and *not war* collapses, and every act of imperial violence can be rationalized as defensive conquest or preemptive self- defense (Aune 2023).

US President Donald Trump's attack on Iran has revived familiar constitutional debates over presidential war powers, ignoring the precedent of the Indian Wars. Liberal progressive Senator Bernie Sanders insisted on X that '[t]here is no ambiguity' that Congress, not the president, decides when the United States goes to war (Sanders 2025). Right-wing libertarian Senator Rand Paul echoed the same point, claiming 'only Congress has the right … to declare war' (Kelly 2025). Trump – who promised to end 'forever wars' and purge the government of 'warmongers and America-last globalists' –now faces criticism even from his own supporters for extending that same imperial project through attacks on Iran and for the US–Israeli genocidal war against Palestinians (Cafiero 2025; Kinnard 2025). Yet such appeals to constitutional procedure and restraint recycle the fiction of a lawful US empire. From the perspective of American Indian studies, these debates obscure the deeper continuity: the US has always waged endless, unilateral wars against those cast outside the protections of 'civilization'.

While bipartisan dissent grows against Trump's foreign policy, American Indian studies scholars remind us that the United States has been fighting 'forever wars' since its founding. 'The Indian wars of the past', wrote Standing Rock Sioux scholar Vine Deloria Jr. in 1969, 'should rightly be regarded as the first foreign wars of American

history' (Deloria 1988[1969], 51). Deloria's point was not radical but historically grounded: the Indian Wars are widely recognized as the legal and political precedent for the US war in Vietnam and present-day wars. These were the first and longest US campaigns – more than a century of undeclared, often genocidal violence – commemorated today in 14 battle streamers on the US Army flag. Drawing out these continuities, the late Dakota scholar Elizabeth Cook-Lynn argued that the US invasions of Iraq under the father-and-son Bush presidencies were not novel departures but extensions of an established pattern. '[N]othing happens on the world stage without context', she observed of the United States' long history of genocidal wars, 'and the old Indian wars are the backdrop for most of the modern events of horror' (Cook-Lynn 2007, 74). Lenape scholar Joanne Barker has likewise traced the roots of modern counterterrorism law and US global policing back to the frontier mythology of 'Red Terror', which cast Native American peoples as irrational aggressors launching unprovoked attacks against innocent white settlers (Barker 2021).

This mythology was enshrined at the nation's founding. In the Declaration of Independence, Thomas Jefferson described Native American nations as 'merciless Indian Savages, whose known rule of warfare, is an undistinguished destruction of all ages, sexes,

and conditions' (United States 1776). Such language was not mere rhetoric. The portrayal of Indigenous violence, or even the threat of it, as irrational and unprovoked, functioned as a standing justification for wars of extermination framed as acts of self-defense, requiring no formal declarations of war. Viewed in this long historical context, the brazen attack on Iran was far from exceptional. Recent US military interventions follow a consistent pattern of unilateral action. In January 2020, the US assassinated Qasem Soleimani, commander of Iran's elite Quds Force, *via* a drone strike at an Iraqi airfield. Earlier, in

2018, Trump launched missile strikes on an alleged Syrian chemical weapons facility, and in 2017 targeted a Syrian airbase – none of which were authorized by federal law. Instead, Trump invoked his constitutional powers as commander in chief, powers whose legal justification, according to proponents, traces back to the earliest US wars against American Indian nations (Corn 2025).

Following the 2018 strikes on Syria, the Department of Justice's Office of Legal Counsel (OLC) – sometimes called the 'Supreme Court of the executive branch' (PBS NewsHour 2014) – opined that the president 'is authorized to commit … hostilities, without prior congressional approval' (U.S. Department of Justice 2018). This claim rested almost entirely on precedent: US presidents had ordered similar actions on 'dozens of occasions over the course of 230years' (U.S. Department of Justice 2018). The OLC noted that 'presidents have exercised their authority to direct military operations without congressional authorization since the earliest days of the Republic', beginning with George Washington's offensive operations against the Wabash Indians in the 1790 war against the Northwest Indian Confederacy, a loose federation of tribes formed to resist US expansion following the Revolutionary War. Quoting legal scholar David P. Currie, the OLC observed that Washington and Secretary of War Henry Knox 'seemed to think [the commander in chief's] authority extended to offensive operations undertaken in retaliation for Indian atrocities' (U.S. Department of Justice 2018). That was the US's first war after drafting its Constitution, and it was highly consequential not only as a legal precedent but also because of how it resulted in one of the worst battlefield defeats of the US military, which was nearly annihilated at the Battle of Wabash in 1791 at the hands of an American Indian army (Grenier 2005, 198–199).

Previous OLC opinions cited the Northwest Indian War as precedent, but none had given it as much attention as the 2018 opinion. Those

earlier opinions drew on the work of legal scholars like John C. Yoo, who derived constitutional lessons from the Northwest Indian War and other Indian wars to justify the expansion of presidential war powers and the use of torture (U.S. Department of Justice 2000; Yoo & Delahunty 2001; U.S. Department of Justice 2003). According to the OLC, this authority – exercised in the Indian Wars – had been invoked repeatedly throughout US history: more than 100 times by the mid-twentieth century, and again in Korea, Vietnam, Libya, Somalia, Bosnia, Haiti, Kosovo, Sudan, Afghanistan, Yemen, Iraq, and beyond (U.S. Department of Justice 2003). In contrast, the US Congress has only declared war 11 times, encompassing 5 separate wars: the War of 1812, the Mexican– American War, the Spanish–American War, and World Wars I and II. And yet, according to the OLC, by the middle of the twentieth century, US presidents had deployed the military for foreign intervention well over 100 times (U.S. Department of Justice 2003).

In this light, the legal justification for contemporary 'forever wars' emerges not from novelty or necessity, but from a centuries-long pattern and practice of unilateral, preemptive, and often extralegal military action originating in the settler-colonial conquest of Indigenous nations. The use of the Indian Wars to justify US imperialist interventions demonstrates a relevance beyond the most recent attacks on Iran, as the targets of those interventions have changed over time, from communist containment strategies during the Cold War to so-called terror wars against the Axis of Resistance, and perhaps future wars to contain the growing influence of China.

The US bombing of Iran during the '12-Day War' quite literally shows the extent to which Israel's genocidal war against Palestinians depends on US military support. The justification for attacking Iran stemmed from the long-running fearmongering that Iran has been developing nuclear weapons. Whether this suspicion is true is beside the point.

Depicting Iranian sovereignty as barbaric and an immortal threat to Israel, and thus US hegemony in the region, casts it outside the realm of civilizational norms (Turfah 2025). When Iranian missiles struck Tel Aviv, causing significant damage, in retaliation to Israeli preemptive attacks, the United States immediately escalated to protect its ally. Trump characterized the US intervention as aiming to stop the 'threat posed by the world's number one state sponsor of terror' (The Associated Press 2025). The intervention was not an isolated act, but part of a broader imperial project aimed at destabilizing regional sovereignty in West Asia, citing the real or imagined threat of Iranian nuclear capabilities. As with the Indian Wars, the enemy is depicted as irrational and barbaric, thereby justifying unilateral US violence as defensive necessity.

This logic extends beyond West Asia. The current deployment of US warships to Venezuela, targeting so-called 'narco-terrorists', and the militarization of the Pacific to counter the rise of China and Asia, more broadly, suggest that the Indian Wars framework continues to structure US global strategy. In each case, adversaries are portrayed as threats to civilization whose very existence outside the US-dominated order warrants preemptive action. The wars may differ in scale and geography, but their justifications remain the same. From the conquest of Indigenous nations to the present, US imperialism has always been an Indian War. Unless stopped, it will remain one.
